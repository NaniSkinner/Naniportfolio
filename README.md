# How I Built and Edited My Portfolio with Perplexity

This README is for anyone who wants to use Perplexity as a creative and technical partner to build or refine a personal portfolio website.

Instead of trying to do everything in one prompt, the best results came from treating the process like a real collaboration: describe what feels off, react to what gets built, share screenshots, give references, and refine step by step.

## The Big Idea

Perplexity worked best when it was treated like:
- a design collaborator
- a front-end builder
- a copy and positioning editor
- a debugging partner
- a deployment guide

The portfolio did **not** come from one perfect prompt. It came from many rounds of feedback.

## What Helped Most

### 1. Start with the current site

The first useful move was sharing the current website and asking what it communicated.

Examples of useful prompts:
- "Here is my site. What story is it telling right now?"
- "What feels missing if I want this to help me get speaking opportunities or engineering roles?"
- "What would you change first?"

This helped identify the gap between the existing site and the site that was actually needed.

## 2. Be clear about the goal of the portfolio

A portfolio can do many things:
- get clients
- help with job opportunities
- support speaking invitations
- establish a personal brand
- show technical credibility

The clearer the goal, the better Perplexity can shape the structure and tone.

Examples of useful prompts:
- "I want this portfolio to position me as a product engineer."
- "I want it to feel warm and editorial, not like a SaaS template."
- "I want it to show that I build AI for real people."

## 3. Build the site section by section

Instead of saying "make me a portfolio," it helped to break the work into sections.

Examples:
- "Rewrite the hero so it sounds more like me."
- "Create a stronger Currently Building section."
- "Make the Products I've Built section feel more polished."
- "Help me clean up the Talks section."

This made the work easier to control and easier to revise.

## 4. Use screenshots constantly

One of the most useful parts of the process was sharing screenshots whenever something looked wrong.

A screenshot is often better than a long explanation because it lets Perplexity see:
- layout problems
- spacing issues
- broken styling
- duplicated numbering
- awkward buttons
- weak visual hierarchy

Examples of useful prompts:
- "The Talks section has extra numbers."
- "This button looks wrong."
- "The products section broke after the animation changes."
- "Here is a screenshot of what I see."

## 5. Use references when you want a visual feeling

When the goal was emotional or stylistic, references helped a lot.

Examples:
- sharing a knowledge graph reference and saying: "I want this feeling"
- sharing a logo image and saying: "Can we get closer to this?"
- sharing a portfolio reference and saying: "I like how this flows downward"

This gave Perplexity a direction that was much more useful than vague words like "make it better" or "make it modern."

## 6. Ask for small changes, not giant rewrites

The best workflow was to make one change at a time.

Examples:
- "Remove the Invite to Speak button."
- "Add my real X account."
- "Replace the email with this one."
- "Make the social links icons only."
- "Add a small signature that says Made with 🍵."

Small changes are easier to evaluate and less likely to break something else.

## 7. Ask for rollback safety before major experiments

This mattered most when changing the knowledge graph.

Before trying a redesign, it helped to say something like:
- "Let's try it, but make sure we can revert if needed."

That made it possible to explore freely without worrying about losing a version that already worked.

## 8. Let Perplexity debug the problems it creates

Sometimes a change caused regressions. That is normal in iterative work.

The important thing was to point directly at the problem and ask for a fix.

Examples:
- "The Products I've Built page got damaged."
- "The animation is not working."
- "The cursor experiment is too much — remove it."

Perplexity worked well as a debugger when given a specific visual or functional issue.

## 9. Ask for explanation when you are ready to deploy

Once the site looked right, the next step was not design — it was publishing.

This is where it helped to ask practical questions like:
- "How do I get this live on my current domain?"
- "Do I need to clone the repo?"
- "Can I do this through GitHub in the browser?"
- "Should this all go in index.html?"

Perplexity can guide deployment, but it helps a lot if you share:
- your Vercel project
- your GitHub repo
- screenshots of what you see in settings

## A Good Prompting Pattern

A simple pattern that worked well throughout this project was:

1. Say what you want.
2. Say what feels wrong right now.
3. Share a screenshot or reference if possible.
4. Ask for one concrete change.
5. Review the result.
6. Repeat.

This works better than trying to design, write, code, and deploy everything in one message.

## Example Prompts That Worked Well

Here are the kinds of prompts that led to useful progress:

- "Here is my current site. What story is it telling?"
- "I want the PRD and then for you to execute it."
- "In the Talks we have extra numbers. Get rid of them."
- "Add my actual X account and LinkedIn."
- "Let's actually get rid of all the words and leave only the logos."
- "At the bottom of all my creations I like to put Made with 🍵."
- "This logo doesn't feel like me. Ask me questions."
- "Take a look at this page and see how it flows downward."
- "Let's research how to get the knowledge graph closer to this reference."
- "Let's try it but make sure we can revert."
- "How do we get this portfolio deployed to my current website?"

## What I Learned

The most important lesson is that Perplexity is strongest when used as an iterative partner, not a one-shot website generator.

The quality came from:
- reacting to what was built
- refining the writing
- using references
- sharing screenshots
- making careful changes
- keeping the ability to revert

That is what made the final result feel specific and personal rather than generic.

## If You Want to Do This Yourself

A practical way to start is:

1. Share your current site.
2. Ask what it communicates now.
3. Explain what you want it to communicate instead.
4. Work section by section.
5. Use screenshots for problems.
6. Use references for visual direction.
7. Ask for backups before big redesigns.
8. Ask for deployment help only after the site is ready.

## Final Advice

Do not worry about writing the perfect prompt.

Just start with what feels wrong, what you want instead, and what kind of person or professional you want the site to represent. If you stay specific and keep the conversation iterative, Perplexity can be a very strong partner in shaping a portfolio.
